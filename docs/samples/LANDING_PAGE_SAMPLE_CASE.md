# 1. Case Summary

## Project Title

```text
Youth AI Literacy Workshop Landing Page Prototype Brief
```

## Fictional Context

A fictional NGO / education programme team wants to promote a beginner-friendly youth AI literacy workshop.

The original idea is vague:

```text
We want to run a youth AI workshop and need a simple page to explain why students, parents, teachers, and youth workers should care.
```

The team needs a landing page / campaign prototype brief before deciding whether to build a real page, create a Canva deck, or use a simple sign-up form.

## Target Audience

Primary audience:

- Secondary school students
- Parents
- Teachers
- Youth workers

Secondary audience:

- NGO programme managers
- School partners
- Funders or internal decision-makers reviewing the programme concept

## Goal

Create a landing page / demo prototype brief that explains:

- what the workshop is
- who it is for
- why AI literacy matters
- what participants will do
- what safe and responsible AI use means
- what action the audience should take next

## Constraints

```text
Time: one-day planning sprint
Budget: low-cost prototype planning only
Data: fake / generic sample data only
Scope: landing page / campaign prototype brief, not a production website
Technical scope: no backend, no real registration system, no analytics setup
Privacy: no real student, parent, teacher, youth worker, SEN, or service-user data
Claims: no real user results, conversion, learning outcome, user testing, or client delivery claim
```

## Expected Deliverable

```text
A Markdown landing page / prototype brief containing:
- problem statement
- value proposition
- page structure
- draft landing page copy
- CTA
- visual direction
- AI-assisted workflow
- validation checklist
- portfolio case note
```

Youth, SEN, education, and social-service contexts require conservative handling.

Default rule:

```text
Do not upload raw student, youth, SEN, service-user, parent, teacher, or programme-participant data to AI tools.
Use anonymized summaries or fake examples first.
```

If the project becomes real, legal / compliance / organizational policy confirmation may be needed depending on the client, sector, data type, and jurisdiction.

Human review is required before:

- sharing the brief with a client or partner
- turning this into a public landing page

# 5. Problem Statement

## Audience Struggle

Students hear about AI every day but may not know how to use it safely, critically, and practically.

Parents, teachers, and youth workers may want young people to develop AI literacy, but they may also worry about:

- misinformation
- overreliance on AI-generated answers
- privacy and data misuse
- academic integrity
- lack of practical beginner-friendly guidance

## Why the Workshop Needs a Landing Page

The workshop idea is useful, but the audience needs a clear explanation before they decide whether to join, refer students, or support the programme.

A landing page helps clarify:

- who the workshop is for
- what participants will actually do
- what responsible AI use means
- what the learning outcomes are, without overpromising
- what action to take next

## Decision the Landing Page Should Support

The landing page should help the visitor decide:

```text
Is this workshop relevant, safe, practical, and worth registering interest for or recommending?
```

## Clear Problem Statement

```text
Secondary school students, parents, teachers, and youth workers need a practical way to understand youth AI literacy because AI tools are becoming common in learning and daily life, but many existing explanations are either too technical, too hype-driven, or unclear about privacy and responsible use.
```

# 6. Value Proposition

## One-line Value Proposition

```text
A beginner-friendly youth AI literacy workshop that helps students understand AI tools, use them responsibly, and think critically before trusting AI-generated answers.
```

## 3 Key Benefits

```text
1. Practical understanding
Participants learn what AI tools can and cannot do through simple examples.

2. Responsible use
Participants discuss privacy, accuracy, overreliance, and human review.

3. Confidence for next steps
Students, parents, teachers, and youth workers gain a shared language for safer AI learning.
```

## Trust / Credibility Notes

Trust should come from clear scope and responsible boundaries, not exaggerated claims.

Possible credibility signals:

- workshop is designed for beginners
- examples use fake or public-safe data
- responsible AI and privacy are included
- teachers / youth workers can review the approach
- no claim that AI replaces educators or professional judgment

## Risk of Overclaiming

Avoid saying:

```text
- Students will master AI in one workshop.
- This workshop guarantees better grades.
- AI will solve all learning problems.
- The programme is proven to improve academic performance.
- Participants will become AI experts after one session.
- This landing page will guarantee sign-ups or conversion.
```

Safer wording:

```text
This workshop introduces practical AI literacy concepts and responsible-use habits for beginners.
```

# 9. Landing Page Structure

This section follows the logic of `docs/LANDING_PAGE_PROTOTYPE_BRIEF.md`.

## Section 1: Hero

Goal:

- explain the workshop in one glance
- show that it is beginner-friendly and safety-aware
- provide a clear CTA

Content:

```text
Headline
Subheadline
Primary CTA
Secondary CTA
Short trust note
```

## Section 2: Problem

Explain the audience pain point:

```text
AI is already part of learning and daily life, but many young people are using it without enough guidance on accuracy, privacy, and responsible judgment.
```

## Section 3: Who It Is For

```text
- Secondary school students curious about AI
- Parents who want to understand how young people use AI
- Teachers exploring AI literacy activities
- Youth workers supporting digital literacy programmes
```

Not for:

```text
- advanced AI engineering training
- exam shortcut coaching
- replacing school policy or professional guidance
- handling real student personal data inside AI tools
```

## Section 4: Workshop Outcomes

Possible outcomes:

```text
By the end of the workshop, participants should be introduced to how to:
1. explain what AI tools can and cannot do in simple terms
2. identify basic risks such as hallucination, privacy leakage, and overreliance
3. use a simple human-review checklist before trusting AI output
4. discuss responsible AI use with peers, teachers, or youth workers
```

Note: these are proposed workshop outcomes, not verified learning results.

## Section 5: What Participants Will Do

```text
- explore simple AI use cases with fake sample prompts
- compare strong and weak AI-generated answers
- identify privacy-safe and unsafe input examples
- create a simple responsible-use checklist
- reflect on when human judgment is needed
```

## Section 6: Why This Matters

```text
AI literacy is not only about knowing which tools exist. It is about learning how to question AI output, protect personal data, and decide when human judgment is needed.
```

## Section 7: Safety / Responsible AI Note

```text
This workshop uses fake or public-safe examples only. Participants should not upload real personal, school, family, health, counselling, financial, identity, or service-user data into AI tools during the session.
```

## Section 8: FAQ

Suggested FAQ topics:

- Is this workshop suitable for beginners?
- Will students need to create AI tool accounts?
- Will real student data be used?
- Is this a coding workshop?
- Can parents or teachers join?
- What should participants prepare?

## Section 9: CTA

Primary CTA:

```text
Register interest
```

Secondary CTA:

```text
Download workshop outline
```

CTA note:

```text
If this becomes a real page, registration data collection must be planned separately with privacy and consent review.
```

# 11. Draft Landing Page Copy

## Headline

```text
Help young people understand AI before they rely on it.
```

## Subheadline

```text
A beginner-friendly AI literacy workshop for secondary students, parents, teachers, and youth workers — focused on practical use, critical thinking, privacy, and responsible human review.
```

## Primary CTA

```text
Register Interest
```

## Secondary CTA

```text
View Workshop Outline
```

## 3 Benefit Bullets

```text
- Learn what AI tools can and cannot do through simple, beginner-friendly examples.
- Practise safer AI habits, including privacy checks and source questioning.
- Build a shared language for students, parents, teachers, and youth workers to discuss responsible AI use.
```

## FAQ Examples

### Is this workshop suitable for complete beginners?

```text
Yes. The workshop is designed for participants with little or no AI background. It focuses on simple examples, safe use, and practical discussion rather than technical theory.
```

### Is this a coding workshop?

```text
No. This is an AI literacy workshop, not a programming course. The focus is on understanding AI tools, asking better questions, checking output, and protecting personal data.
```

### Will real student data be used?

```text
No. The workshop should use fake or public-safe examples only. Participants should not upload personal, school, family, health, counselling, financial, identity, or service-user data into AI tools.
```

### Can parents, teachers, or youth workers join?

```text
Yes. The workshop is designed to create a shared foundation for young people and the adults who support them.
```

### What should participants prepare?

```text
Participants only need curiosity and basic internet access. If a real AI tool is used during the session, account requirements and privacy guidance should be confirmed before the workshop.
```

# 12. Visual Direction

## Tone

```text
Practical, calm, youth-friendly, responsible, non-hype, education-focused.
```

Avoid:

```text
- futuristic fear-based visuals
- exaggerated robot imagery
- “AI will change everything overnight” messaging
- exam shortcut or productivity miracle language
```

## Suggested Imagery

Use generic or licensed visuals such as:

- students discussing ideas in a workshop setting
- abstract AI / digital literacy illustrations
- teacher or youth worker facilitating a discussion
- privacy checklist or human-review checklist diagrams
- simple cards showing “question”, “check”, “review”, “decide”

Do not use:

- real student photos without consent
- identifiable school photos
- screenshots with account names or personal data
- copyrighted competitor visuals
- unlicensed icons, fonts, music, or images

## Layout Notes

Suggested structure:

```text
1. Hero with clear CTA
2. Problem explanation
3. Who this is for
4. Workshop outcomes
5. Activity preview
6. Responsible AI note
7. FAQ
8. Final CTA
```

Keep the page short enough for mobile reading.

## Accessibility Notes

```text
- Use clear headings.
- Avoid dense paragraphs.
- Use readable font size.
- Maintain strong color contrast.
- Make CTA buttons easy to tap on mobile.
- Avoid relying only on images to explain key information.
- Use plain language for parents and youth workers.
```

## Copyright / Asset Licensing Reminder

Competitor pages may be used only for structure learning and positioning comparison.

Do not copy:

- exact text
- visual layout
- photos
- illustrations
- logos
- testimonials
- workshop claims
- proprietary programme names

All visual assets should be owned, licensed, or approved for the intended use.

# 13. AI-assisted Workflow

## How AI Could Help

AI can support this project by helping to:

```text
1. Summarize source notes
Turn rough workshop notes into a structured brief.

2. Draft copy
Generate first-draft headline, subheadline, FAQ, and benefit bullets.

3. Generate alternative CTA
Compare options such as Register Interest, View Workshop Outline, Ask About School Session.

4. Create FAQ
Draft beginner-friendly answers for parents, teachers, students, and youth workers.

5. Check tone
Review whether the copy sounds too technical, too promotional, or too vague.

6. Create portfolio summary
Convert the workflow into a portfolio case note, CV bullet, LinkedIn post, and interview pitch.
```

## What Must Remain Human-reviewed

Human review is required for:

```text
- workshop accuracy
- age suitability
- privacy and consent language
- claims about learning outcomes
- use of NGO / school / partner names
- use of photos, logos, and testimonials
- final CTA and data collection method
- accessibility and youth-friendly wording
- portfolio publication permission
```

AI should not decide whether a claim is legally, medically, educationally, or institutionally safe. Human and client approval is required.
